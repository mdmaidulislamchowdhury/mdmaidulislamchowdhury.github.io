About

I am a PhD candidate in the Department of Economics at the University of Calgary. My research lies in applied macroeconomics, with a particular focus on monetary economics. I study how alternative measures of money influence key questions in monetary economics, including money demand, the velocity of money, and the transmission of monetary policy. My work makes extensive use of the Divisia monetary aggregates produced by the Center for Financial Stability to examine whether they provide more informative measures of liquidity than conventional monetary aggregates. I am also interested in incorporating monetary variables into the study of business cycle facts to better understand how different definitions of money shape macroeconomic fluctuations.

I hold an MA in Economics from the University of South Carolina. Before beginning my doctoral studies, I worked in the Research Department of the Bangladesh Bank, where I conducted research on monetary policy, financial markets, and the Bangladesh economy.

Working Papers

Friedman's Money Supply Volatility Hypothesis and the Quantity Theory of Money: The Evidence from the Di⁄erent Types of Money

Abstract

This paper tests the Milton Friedman hypothesis that money growth variability affects the velocity of money. It compares the Federal Reserve’s simple-sum monetary aggregates, the original (non-credit-card-augmented) Divisia aggregates, and the credit-augmented Divisia and credit-augmented Divisia inside monetary aggregates using two classes of empirical models. Standard Granger causality tests and a bivariate structural GARCH-in-Mean VAR model examine how money growth variability affects the consumption and income velocity of money. Using monthly data for the post-2006 period, when all monetary indicators are available, the findings reject the Friedman hypothesis when money supply volatility is modeled explicitly using recent advances in macroeconometrics and financial econometrics. This result is robust to a longer sample extending back to 1967 with monthly and quarterly data, across alternative model specifications. From a policy standpoint, this enhances the predictability and reliability of monetary policy actions aimed at stabilizing the economy using monetary aggregates.

(with Apostolos Serletis)
